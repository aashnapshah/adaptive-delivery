§1.1 · Motivation

The constraint is delivery, not diagnosis

  • Reasoning LLMs now match or exceed physicians on diagnostic tasks (Brodeur '26, Kanjee '23, Nori '25)
  • Goh 2024 (RCT): the model alone beat physicians — yet physicians given the model did not improve
  • The capability was available and went unused
  • Indiscriminate, interruptive alerts → alert fatigue → overrides (AHRQ, Baron, Park)
3
§1.2 · Prior work

Nori et al. (2025): SDBench + MAI-DxO

  • SDBench turns 304 NEJM CPC cases into interactive sequential encounters — ask, order, or diagnose through a gatekeeper
  • MAI-DxO: an orchestrator that role-plays a panel of five physician personas in one model
  • On o3: ~80% accuracy vs. ~20% for generalist physicians, at lower cumulative test cost
  • A strong foundation — but four gaps for the question of clinical impact
4
§1.2 · Four gaps we address

From a diagnostic benchmark to clinical impact

  • Ends at diagnosis — models generation, not whether it's delivered or acted on → we add scoring, delivery, response & evaluation
  • One regime (rare CPC) → three regimes: CPC, MIMIC-IV (acute), Clalit (routine)
  • Triple Aim omits clinician workload/experience → addressed via the delivery layer
  • Closed frontier model → open models & public data throughout
5
§1.3 · Research question

Not just whether a recommendation is right — how it's delivered

Can adaptive, context-aware delivery raise appropriate clinician uptake without adding alert burden?

§2.6 · Evaluation metrics

What we measure — automatic vs. clinician-graded

  • Diagnostic quality: accuracy (share scored ≥4) + mean Judge score
  • Cost & effort: a $300 visit fee + a per-test price schedule; turns, tests, tokens, latency
  • Ordering concordance (MIMIC/Clalit): recall & precision vs. the labs actually measured
  • Appropriateness & harm: clinician-graded (NOHARM-style prototype scorer, kept hidden)
  • Ablations & a budget sweep probe the panel and how designs degrade under cost caps

§3.1 · Why the panel underperforms

It commits early instead of testing

  • The panel commits after ~6 turns (not the full 8) and orders ~0.4 tests/case (vs. ~6.5)
  • On an 8B model the coordinator's structured output often fails to parse → it falls back and commits
  • Example (West Nile): it had WNV-positive CSF in hand at turn 2 — but committed “undetermined” (Judge 1/5)
  • Its low dollar cost reflects early commitment, not efficient work-up

§3.3 · Evaluation in progress

Instrumented, not yet informative

  • Panel ablation (leave-one-persona-out): needs a capable model to separate personas
  • Budget sweep (accuracy under cost ceilings): curves pending a capable model
  • Ordering concordance: MIMIC-IV cohort built (98 cases); sweep in progress; Clalit pending

§4.2 · The novel contribution

An adaptive delivery layer

Once generation is solid, learn how to present each scored recommendation — interruptive alert, passive display, or suppress — to raise appropriate uptake without adding alert burden.
  • Context-aware: the same recommendation surfaced differently in a busy ED vs. a low-load clinic
  • Calibrated & validated with a human study on real clinician response

Summary

Contributions & the takeaway

  • Contributions: the first study of the delivery of LM clinical recommendations; reusable sequential test-ordering environments from real EHR data; a safety-grounded, multi-objective alerting layer reproducible on public data
  • Finding so far: on an open 8B model, added orchestration does not buy accuracy — a single call matches the roles prompt, and the multi-agent panel is weaker at ~1.9× the compute
  • Next: frontier models toward ~80%, then the adaptive delivery layer
